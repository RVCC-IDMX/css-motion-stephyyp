Motion Board

Three experiments exploring motion and accessibility with CSS

Section 1: Micro Interaction

This micro interaction has a calm, responsive personality. On hover and focus, the button gently lifts and slightly increases its opacity over a 300ms transform-based transition, so it feels alive and interactive without being loud or distracting.

Section 2: Timing Comparison

Ease-out timing

Custom cubic-bezier

Both buttons move over the same duration, but their timing curves create very different feelings. The ease-out button feels smooth and predictable, slowing down gently as it finishes the motion. The custom cubic-bezier button feels more playful and energetic, with a sharper acceleration that gives the interaction a bit of “bounce,” even though the distance and duration are the same.

Section 3: Feature Card (3D Flip)

Card Front: Feature Overview

The front of this card gives a quick overview of the feature. Hover or focus to flip and reveal more detailed information on the back.

Card Back: Details

The back of the card expands on the idea with more explanation. The 3D flip uses a 500ms transform-based rotation so the layout stays stable while only the card content changes in place.

Section 4: Accessibility & Reflection

Reduced Motion Implementation

Across all three sections, I used the prefers-reduced-motion media query to turn off animated transitions for users who request reduced motion. When that preference is enabled, elements still change state, but they do so instantly or with very minimal change to opacity instead of animated transforms. This keeps the interface usable while avoiding potentially uncomfortable motion.

Design Reflection

Building this motion board helped me see how much timing and easing curves change the “personality” of an interaction, even when the distance and duration stay the same. I was surprised by how subtle adjustments in the cubic-bezier curve could make a button feel calm, snappy, or playful.

The 3D feature card is the demo that best expresses my intent, because it feels dynamic and modern without breaking the layout or overwhelming the rest of the page. Overall, I learned that motion works best when it supports clarity and feedback first, and only then adds style and personality on top of a solid, accessible foundation.
